Sensors: How Space Robots See and Navigate

Space robots rely on a sophisticated array of sensors to navigate, analyse their surroundings, and conduct scientific experiments in extreme environments where humans cannot go.

Camera Systems

What they do: Multiple cameras capture 3D stereo images and colour photos

How robots use them: Navigate around obstacles, identify interesting rocks or targets, and create detailed terrain maps. Perseverance has 23 cameras!

Temperature Sensors

What they do: Monitor temperatures of the robot and environment

How robots use them: Mars rovers use them to survive -125¬∞C nights and protect sensitive electronics from extreme temperature changes

Spectrometers

What they do: Analyse the chemical composition of rocks and soil

How robots use them: Identify minerals, detect water signatures, and search for organic compounds that could indicate past life

Gyroscopes & Accelerometers

What they do: Measure rotation and acceleration

How robots use them: Know which direction they're facing, how far they've travelled, and maintain balance on rocky terrain

Radio Antennas

What they do: Send and receive signals to/from Earth

How robots use them: Download instructions, upload scientific data and images, and determine precise position using signals from orbiting satellites

Radiation Detectors

What they do: Measure radiation levels

How robots use them: Assess radiation hazards for future human missions and study cosmic rays hitting the planet's surface

Working together, these sensors give space robots a detailed understanding of their environment, enabling them to make autonomous decisions millions of kilometres from Earth!

Sensors: How Boston Dynamics Robots Achieve Amazing Balance

Boston Dynamics robots are famous for their incredible balance and agility. This is made possible by a sophisticated network of sensors working together at lightning speed.

Inertial Measurement Units (IMUs)

What they do: Measure orientation, acceleration, and angular velocity

How robots use them: Constantly monitor which way is "up" and detect if the robot is tipping over, allowing instant corrections to maintain balance

Joint Position Sensors

What they do: Track the angle and position of every joint

How robots use them: Know exactly where each leg or arm is, enabling precise movements and adjustments during complex manoeuvres like backflips

Force/Torque Sensors

What they do: Measure forces on the robot's feet or hands

How robots use them: Detect contact with the ground or objects, adjust grip strength, and distribute weight properly when landing from jumps

Depth Cameras

What they do: Create 3D maps of the environment

How robots use them: See obstacles, measure distances, plan where to step next, and avoid collisions when navigating complex spaces

LiDAR (Spot)

What they do: Use laser pulses to measure distances

How robots use them: Create precise 360¬∞ maps, navigate in dark environments, and detect obstacles that cameras might miss

Motor Current Sensors

What they do: Monitor how hard each motor is working

How robots use them: Detect when a leg hits an obstacle or when the robot is pushing against something, enabling quick reactions

All these sensors update hundreds of times per second, allowing Boston Dynamics robots to react faster than human reflexes and maintain balance in situations that would make humans fall over!

Sensors: Achieving Surgical Precision

Surgical robots use extremely precise sensors to give surgeons superhuman control, translating large hand movements into tiny, tremor-free actions inside the patient's body.

High-Definition 3D Cameras

What they do: Capture stereoscopic images inside the body

How robots use them: Give surgeons a 3D view with 10x magnification, showing depth and detail impossible to see with the naked eye

Position Encoders

What they do: Track the exact position of every joint and instrument

How robots use them: Translate surgeon's hand movements into precise instrument movements, scaling down motions by up to 5:1

Force Feedback Sensors

What they do: Measure resistance when instruments touch tissue

How robots use them: Help surgeons "feel" how much pressure they're applying, preventing damage to delicate organs and blood vessels

Motion Scaling & Tremor Filtration

What they do: Monitor surgeon's hand movements for tremors

How robots use them: Filter out hand tremors and scale down movements, so a 1cm hand motion becomes a 0.2cm instrument motion

Instrument Identification

What they do: Recognise which surgical tool is attached

How robots use them: Automatically adjust movement ranges and behaviours based on the tool (scissors vs. cautery vs. grasper)

Collision Detection

What they do: Monitor for potential collisions between robotic arms

How robots use them: Prevent arms from bumping into each other inside the patient, ensuring safe multi-arm operations

Together, these sensors enable surgeons to perform delicate procedures with sub-millimetre precision, making operations safer and recovery faster for patients!

Sensors: Navigating the Deep Ocean

Underwater robots face unique challenges - GPS doesn't work underwater, visibility is often zero, and pressure increases dramatically with depth. Here's how sensors help them navigate these extreme conditions.

Sonar (Sound Navigation)

What they do: Send out sound waves and measure their echoes

How robots use them: "See" in murky or dark water, create maps of the seafloor, detect obstacles, and measure distances when visibility is zero

Pressure Sensors

What they do: Measure water pressure

How robots use them: Calculate depth precisely (every 10 metres of depth adds one atmosphere of pressure), helping robots maintain specific depths for surveys

Inertial Navigation System

What they do: Track position using gyroscopes and accelerometers

How robots use them: Navigate without GPS by tracking every movement, measuring rotation and acceleration to calculate position relative to starting point

HD Cameras & Lighting

What they do: Capture visual images with powerful LED lights

How robots use them: Document discoveries, identify species, inspect structures, and enable human operators to see what the robot sees

Temperature & Salinity Sensors

What they do: Measure water temperature and salt content

How robots use them: Study ocean currents, identify different water masses, and understand marine ecosystems - different species prefer different temperatures

Doppler Velocity Log

What they do: Measure speed over the seafloor using sound

How robots use them: Track how fast and in which direction they're moving, helping maintain precise positioning during surveys or inspections

These sensors work together to help underwater robots navigate and explore the ocean depths, where over 80% of our planet's oceans remain unexplored!

Sensors: How Farm Robots See Crops

Agricultural robots must distinguish ripe from unripe produce, identify weeds among crops, and navigate unpredictable outdoor environments. Here's how their sensors make precision farming possible.

RGB & Depth Cameras

What they do: Capture colour images and measure 3D depth

How robots use them: Identify fruits by colour (red strawberry vs green), measure size, locate produce in 3D space, and guide robotic arms precisely to each item

AI Vision Systems

What they do: Process images using machine learning

How robots use them: Distinguish crops from weeds, assess ripeness, identify plant diseases, and make split-second picking decisions - trained on millions of images

GPS & RTK Systems

What they do: Provide centimetre-accurate positioning

How robots use them: Navigate fields autonomously, return to exact locations, ensure seeds are planted in perfectly straight rows, and avoid going over the same area twice

Multispectral Sensors

What they do: Detect light beyond human vision (infrared, ultraviolet)

How robots use them: Assess plant health invisible to the eye, detect water stress before visible symptoms, measure chlorophyll levels, and spot pest infestations early

Tactile Force Sensors

What they do: Measure grip pressure and firmness

How robots use them: Apply just enough force to pick fruit without bruising, detect if produce is too soft or too firm, and adjust grip for different crop sizes

Environmental Sensors

What they do: Monitor temperature, humidity, soil moisture

How robots use them: Optimise spraying times, determine irrigation needs, assess ideal harvesting conditions, and collect data for farmers to improve future yields

These sensors enable agricultural robots to perform tasks with superhuman precision, reducing waste and helping feed our growing global population sustainably!

How Warehouse Automation Works

The Journey of Your Package

  1. Order Placed: Computer system locates the item in the warehouse
  2. Robot Retrieves: Mobile robot navigates to the shelf and brings it to a human picker
  3. Picking: Human or robotic arm selects the correct item
  4. Packing: Item is placed in a box, sometimes by robots
  5. Sorting: Robotic arms sort packages onto conveyor belts by destination
  6. Loading: Packages are loaded onto trucks for delivery

Smart Technology

  • Computer vision to identify products
  • Machine learning to optimise routes
  • QR codes or markers for navigation
  • Real-time coordination of hundreds of robots

Impact on Delivery

  • Same-day and next-day delivery possible
  • Reduced shipping costs
  • Fewer errors in orders
  • Faster during peak times (holidays)

Sensors: Coordinating Hundreds of Robots

Warehouse robots must navigate crowded spaces, avoid collisions with other robots and humans, and find their way through massive facilities. Here's how sensors enable this complex choreography.

QR Code/Barcode Scanners

What they do: Read QR codes on floors or shelves

How robots use them: Navigate to precise locations, identify which shelf to retrieve, verify they're picking the right item, and track their position in the warehouse

LiDAR Sensors

What they do: Use laser pulses to create 2D/3D maps

How robots use them: Detect obstacles in real-time, avoid collisions with other robots or people, measure distances to shelves, and navigate without following floor markers

Computer Vision Cameras

What they do: Capture and analyse visual images

How robots use them: Identify products, read labels, detect humans to slow down or stop, recognise which items to pick, and inspect packages for damage

Weight Sensors

What they do: Measure the mass of items or shelves

How robots use them: Verify the correct item was picked, ensure shelves aren't overloaded, detect if a product is missing, and prevent picking empty containers

Wireless Communication

What they do: Connect robots to central computer system

How robots use them: Receive instructions about which items to fetch, report their location and status, coordinate with other robots to avoid traffic jams, and update the system when tasks are complete

Battery Monitoring

What they do: Track battery charge levels

How robots use them: Know when to return to charging stations, predict remaining working time, schedule charging during slow periods, and prevent running out of power mid-task

These sensors work together with sophisticated software to coordinate hundreds of robots simultaneously, enabling them to work safely alongside humans and process millions of packages every day!
